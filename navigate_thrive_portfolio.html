This is not a research paper—it's your foundation. A jumping-off point for you to think critically about topics that matter to your life right now: what it means to be a Black youth in elite spaces, how to build community, protect your mental health, claim your power, and lead with authenticity.

Use this guide to explore, question, and build the knowledge that's uniquely yours.

Digital Spaces & Identity

We Built the Internet. Now We Run It.

The Black TikTok Phenomenon

28% of Black teens spend time on TikTok "almost constantly," according to Pew Research 2024. But this isn't just about social media. It's about cultural production, digital entrepreneurship, and reshaping narrative.

$15 billion+ in economic value generated by Black creators across social platforms.

#BlackTikTok and #HillmanTok represent decentralized classrooms where Gen Z is teaching itself history, culture, and resistance—outside traditional systems.

Code-Switching Online vs. Offline

You exist in multiple spaces simultaneously—a carefully curated self at Buckley, an authentic self online, and the code-switching in between. Research shows that this constant negotiation of identity creates real cognitive load and stress.

The data is clear: Online spaces can buffer against offline discrimination and isolation. They can also amplify it. Understanding which spaces serve you is critical.

Creator Spotlight: Kahlil Greene (@kahlilgreene)

639K+ followers | "The Gen Z Historian"

Kahlil uses TikTok to teach Black history, call out cultural appropriation, and build community around authentic representation. He's proof that social platforms aren't just for entertainment—they're for education, leadership, and movement-building.

Reflection: What would it look like to use your digital presence as a platform for leadership? What stories are you telling?

Your Reflection Questions:
  • How do you show up differently online vs. at Buckley? Is that intentional or reactive?
  • What digital communities make you feel most like yourself?
  • If you had 10K followers, what would you want to teach or share?
  • How does social media impact your sense of identity and belonging?

Mental Health & Resilience

We Don't Just Survive. We Thrive.

Meet Dr. Jasmin Brooks Stephens (UC Berkeley ESSENCE Lab)

Forbes 30 Under 30 Healthcare 2025 | Leading research on Black youth mental health & suicide prevention

Dr. Stephens' breakthrough research links racial discrimination directly to increased suicide capability among Black adults. But more importantly, her work centers Black youth thriving through six protective factors.

The Black Youth Thriving Framework (6 Protective Factors)

  • Intrapersonal Assets: Your beliefs in yourself, resilience, coping strategies
  • Kinship Support: Family, close relationships, people who know your full self
  • Collective Socialization: Community that teaches you about your culture, history, power
  • Black Cultural Identity: Pride, knowledge, connection to heritage
  • Hope & Future Orientation: Belief that better futures are possible for you and your community
  • Religious/Spiritual Coping: Practices that ground, center, and sustain you

The question for you: Which of these factors are strongest in your life right now? Which need attention?

Code-Switching as Chronic Stress

The science is clear: constantly navigating between authentic self and adapted self creates cognitive load, hypervigilance, and measurable health impacts—linked to heart disease, stroke, and diabetes in adult Black populations.

At an elite prep school, you may find yourself code-switching daily. This is real work. It requires real protection.

Civic Power & Education Policy

50 Million Strong. Use the Power.

The Numbers: Gen Z Political Power in 2026

49 million Gen Z youth are eligible to vote in 2026 midterms.
5.7 million of those are Black youth.
34% Black youth turnout in 2024 (vs. 55% White, 43% Asian)—but 89% say they're willing to vote.

The real story: The gap isn't interest. It's economic barriers—time, transportation, childcare, work. When we remove those barriers, Black youth show up.

PWI & HBCU Landscape

One of the Few. None of the Less.

The Post-SFFA Collapse (June 29, 2023)

When the Supreme Court overturned affirmative action, elite college enrollment of Black students plummeted overnight:

Harvard: 18% → 11.5%
Princeton: 9% → 5% (lowest since 1968)
Amherst: 11% → 6%
Caltech: 5% → 1.6%

Only 2 of 29 elite institutions maintain ≥10% Black enrollment. 11 are now at ≤5%.

This is the landscape you're preparing for. Understanding it matters.

5 PWI Mental Health Risk Factors (Research-Based)

  • Microaggressions: Daily subtle racism, code-switching, hypervigilance
  • Racial Isolation: Being one of few, eating at separate tables, social exclusion
  • Impostor Syndrome: "Did I really earn this?" pressure to prove yourself constantly
  • White Institutional Presence: Faculty, curriculum, norms built for and by white people
  • Lack of Resources: No affinity groups, culturally competent counseling, financial aid

HBCU Profiles: Six Institutions to Know

Howard University (Washington, DC)

Founded: 1867 | Students: 11,519 | 6-yr graduation: 60%

Why it matters: 130K+ alumni network. VP Harris, Thurgood Marshall, Toni Morrison, Chadwick Boseman graduated here. DC Capitol access for internships and policy work.

200+ student orgs | Deep ties to Black political leadership

Spelman College (Atlanta, GA)

Founded: 1881 | Students: 2,300 | Graduation Rate: 75% (HIGHEST HBCU)

Why it matters: #1 HBCU (18 consecutive years). 34% STEM majors. Leading producer of Black women STEM PhDs. DoD Center of Excellence for Minority Women in STEM.

Sister school network with Morehouse creates powerful community

Morehouse College (Atlanta, GA)

Founded: 1867 | Students: 3,000+ | 6-yr graduation: 53%

Why it matters: ONLY all-male HBCU. MLK Jr., Jesse Jackson, Spike Lee graduated here. 33 majors. Future Teachers Excellence Fellowship (~160 fellows annually).

Deep brotherhood culture | Atlanta location (tech hub, companies recruiting)

NC A&T (Greensboro, NC)

Founded: 1891 | Students: 15,275 | Graduation Rate: 64% (↑ from 54%)

Why it matters: FIRST HBCU EVER TO HIT 15K+ STUDENTS. 12th consecutive year as nation's largest HBCU. #1 producer Black engineers & agricultural scientists.

NEW: Bachelor's in Artificial Intelligence (only HBCU). $90M Engineering Research & Innovation Center. Partnerships with Toyota, JetZero.

83.7% employed by graduation | $54.9K mean starting salary

Hampton University (Hampton, VA)

Founded: 1868 | Students: 4,686 | Graduation Rate: 64% (↑ from 54%)

Why it matters: 46% enrollment surge since 2022 (post-SFFA). Carnegie R2 Classification 2024 (only private university in Virginia). 100+ student orgs. ROTC (Army, Navy, Marine).

School of Pharmacy expansion | Vibrant waterfront campus

Fisk University (Nashville, TN)

Founded: 1866 | Students: ~900 | Type: Intimate liberal arts

Why it matters: Historic Jubilee Singers. Hidden gem for close mentoring. Deep faculty-student relationships. Strong alumni network in music, education, activism.

HBCU Surge Post-SFFA: Howard +79% applications, Shaw +36% enrollment, Morehouse 300 over capacity, Hampton +46% overall.

Student Voices & Reflections

Hear From Peers Navigating Spaces Like Yours

These are real reflections from Black students who have navigated elite prep schools, PWIs, and spaces where they were "one of the few." Their stories are different, but the themes are consistent. Read them. Sit with them. Ask yourself the same questions.

"As quickly as the weather changed, I began to feel isolated, an African American woman at a predominantly white institution. The student body is 82 percent white. African Americans accounted for just 2.6 percent. Of course, I'd known the school was mostly white, but that hadn't fazed me. I was eager to meet new people with different backgrounds. What I hadn't counted on was how much the school didn't feel like a place where I could really fit in... It felt like I was starring in my own Netflix episode of 'Dear White People' every day."

— Black woman, PWI (NDSU)

Ask yourself: How does this mirror or differ from your experience at Buckley so far? When do you feel like you're "on" vs. relaxed?

"Being Black at a PWI and wanting to be around peers of color means having to work harder to feel comfortable and accepted in spaces. You have to find those organizations yourself, make those connections and be okay with taking up space in areas where not many people look like you."

— Black student, Ball State University

Ask yourself: What communities or organizations at Buckley help you feel like yourself? If they don't exist, what would it take to create them?

"I have to be open to questions about my culture or my hair or something, and not put up a hard front. This prepared me for the worst-case scenario. When in history class you're talking about slavery, everyone will instantly look at you. Or you're talking about civil rights, everyone is like, 'What do you have to say about it?' It's exhausting to be tokenized, but I learned to set boundaries."

— Black student, Elite Prep School

Ask yourself: When are you asked to speak "for your people"? How do you respond? What boundaries do you want to set?

"My experience as a Black student at PWIs has been a struggle, but ultimately I have learned to be comfortable in my own skin regardless of my surroundings. Going to a PWI taught me to be loud in places where others thought I should be quiet. It taught me to stand up for what I believe in, despite who might be against me."

— Black college student

Ask yourself: What would it take for you to feel comfortable being yourself in spaces where you're the minority? What support do you need?

"The diversity on campus is overwhelmingly lackluster. The lack of diversity is represented in the spaces of the community and causes the voices of minorities to often go unheard. But I also found real friendships here with people who actually saw me. It's complicated."

— Black student, Elite LA Prep School (similar to Buckley)

Ask yourself: How do you experience diversity at Buckley? Who truly sees you there? What do you need to feel heard?

"You've always been the top of the class and now you're average. That's a big shock. But the big hurdle is learning to get the most out of the experience and not let it change who you are—not let the experience make you feel less than anyone else."

— Black student entering elite prep school

Ask yourself: Have you experienced this shift—feeling smaller or less in academic/social spaces? How do you reclaim your sense of worth?

"At my former day school, you could barely make a community out of all the minorities. But at my HBCU, we have the Latino community and a Black community. Compared to my prep school, I was sheltered. Now I've been exposed to so much. I've tried different things. There are so many researchers and opportunities. I've done a lot more than just hit the books."

— Black college student, HBCU transfer from prep school

Ask yourself: What would it feel like to be in a space where being Black is the norm, not the exception? Does that appeal to you? What would you gain? Give up?
